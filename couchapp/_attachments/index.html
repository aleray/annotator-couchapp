Once upon a time there was school

There is no time. We have no time.

What does it mean to say: there is no time, no place, not a thing, we no longer have time, places or things. According to us, statements such as these not only signal that every-thing, and every time and space, tends to be occupied, but also that there is or can be time, space and matter that is not occupied. And for that, we simply want to reserve the notion ‘school’. Perhaps today, especially today when it seems to disappear, we still remember what it is to have free time.

School is literally a place of scholè, that is the space of ‘free time’. 1 Originated in the Greek world, school was not a place and time organised to reproduce social order, or the life form of its elites. Disconnected from both the oikos and polis, and hence freed from daily, economic and political occupations, the school was a real space with a real inner place and time, where people were exposed to real things. Our thesis is that school time, place and matter is actually an invention, it includes a particular form that does certain things, and actually creates a particular time, space and matter. As a form, school includes at least three components.

First, a typical feature of the school is suspension. Economic, social, cultural, political or private time is suspended, as well as the tasks and roles connected to specific places. Suspension here could be regarded as an act of de-privatization or de-appropriation. The school offers students for instance the opportunity to leave behind their past and family background, and indeed to become students like all the rest. And a similar suspension from the side of teachers (a profession that is not really a serious profession), and from the side of subject matter (knowledge and other things that are not for real). We are not claiming that this is what happens in education today – quite the contrary, there is the ongoing tendency to link students to their background for instance, to professionalise teaching or to make subject matter more relevant. The point however is that a school implies an act of suspension or setting free, and indeed, perhaps, that suspension is no longer part of education today.

However, the term ‘free’ has not only the negative meaning of suspension (free from), but also a positive meaning, that is, free to. Drawing upon the terminology of Giorgio Agamben, we want to introduce the term profanation to describe this kind of freedom as the second feature of the school form. According to Agamben2: “Pure, profane, freed from sacred names is that thing that is being replaced in view of the common use by people.” (p. 96) A condition of profane time, space and material thus is not a place of emptiness, but a condition where time, space and things are disconnected from their regular use, no longer scared or occupied in that sense, and hence it refers to a condition where something of the world is open for common use. Knowledge for example, but also skills, that have a particular function in society, become free for common use; disconnected from the usages of the old generation in society but not yet appropriated by students as representatives of the young generation. But important, it is in front of common things – open for free and thus new use – that the young generation is offered the opportunity to experience itself as a new generation, i.e. the experience of potentiality in front of some-thing. For that reason, at school it is always partly about knowledge for the sake of knowledge (what could be called ‘study’), or skills for the sake of skills (what could be called ‘exercise’), and thought for the sake of thought (what could be called ‘thinking’). And the consequence is not that the school is disconnected from society (an ‘ivory tower’, ‘island’, ‘unworldly’). The knowledge, skills and any other material of course are derived from society, but there is the simple but far reaching act of suspension and profanation. And that makes the school perhaps not a societal institution, but a worldly space and time.

The school (through the teacher, school discipline and architecture) makes it possible for the new generation to become attentive for some-thing, that is, for the world. Hence, the third feature we want to mention here is that the school makes attentive. In that sense, subjects or things – disconnected from particular usages and positions - become very real at school. Of course, not as a kind of resource, product or any other object that is occupied and part of a particular economy. The magical event of the school is that and when it turns matter into some-thing, that is, something to study or to exercise3. School than becomes a space and time of inter-esse, and thus, where the young generation is not approached as those who have (specific, individual) needs and want to choose, but as those who are exposed to the world and are offered the opportunity to become interested in some-thing. The school is not about calculation and choices, but about attention and becoming interested in some-thing.

In sum, the school could be regarded as a particular medium, a means without ends, that is a free place and time where something is offered, and without this offering establishing a particular destination or orientation 4. Scholè, than, is not simply a time/space of passage (from past to future), project-time or initiation-time (from family to society). It is the time and space of attention which is the time of the regard for the world, of being present to it (or being in its presence), attending it, a time of delivery to the experience of the world, of exposure and effacing social subjectivities and orientations, a time filled with encounters and opportunities to study and exercise. Taking the swimmer who traverses the stream it could look like the swimmer simply goes from one land to another (from the land of ignorance to that of knowledge for example), as if the medium would be simply a point without dimension (as when we jump) 5 . Of course, she ‘arrives’ in a second world, but more importantly, the swimmer did not only change river banks, but has known the trait that binds them and that is in fact a ‘place’ that integrates all directions, a ‘milieu’ that has no orientation itself, or, the other way round, opens to all directions and orientations.

The dis-appearance of the school-form(s) as a form of suspension, profanation and attention that makes ‘free time’ is not just a far hypothesis but a real challenge to reinvent or shape a school form, that is, to make free time, space and matter. There is maybe one more development that besides the tendencies we already mentioned, strongly challenges this reinvention: ICT and the related creation of so-called digital learning environments and technology enhanced learning. Indeed the question is ever more strong: whether and how to make or sustain scholè in a time of information and communication technologies, of digital technologization and of globalization and the instant? What about the school form in, to draw on Bauman6, a liquid time? What about attention to things and the world in cyberspace? Maybe the virtual can function as a new way to make things real, to let them speak, to provoke thinking. And maybe there are new school forms today, but outside the field of education and learning as we know it.
